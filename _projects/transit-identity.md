A transit identity system built around one constraint: it has to work at 80km/h. Passengers reading a sign from a moving vehicle don't have time for nuance — every element has to land in under a second.

## Concept

The brief started as a wayfinding exercise and became something more involved. Once you start designing for legibility at speed, every decision gets stress-tested in a way that most identity work doesn't. Type size, contrast ratio, spacing, icon weight — nothing is decorative, everything is load-bearing.

### Color

The orange was chosen for contrast against the concrete and steel environments transit infrastructure lives in. Not warm for warmth's sake — warm because it reads at distance, in low light, against a grey platform wall at 6am.

### Typography

Syne for station names and primary wayfinding. IBM Plex Mono for line numbers, platform codes, and time displays — the data layer that needs to be scannable rather than readable.

## Process

The system was developed through a series of simulated viewing conditions: printed at various sizes, photographed from distance, tested in low-contrast environments. What looked good on screen often failed in the field. The orange was darkened twice before it held reliably against a white background in direct sunlight.

### Signage hierarchy

Three levels: primary (station name, line color), secondary (platform, direction), tertiary (service info, connections). Each level has a fixed type size and contrast ratio — nothing overrides the hierarchy for aesthetic reasons.

### Motion

Applied to digital displays on platforms and in carriages. The motion system uses the same timing as the identity — fast in, hold, fast out. Nothing lingers. The interface assumes the passenger is always about to leave.
