This is me. Not a studio, not a brand exercise. Just something I made to represent how I should visually appear and what I care about, built around a visual obsession I've had since I was a kid.

## Where it comes from

There's a specific kind of design from the early 2000s that I never really got over. Not the grunge era, not the maximalist stuff. The other thing. Elements like The main menu of Ridge Racer 4. Gran Turismo 4's car selection. Those DVD interfaces that looked like they were designed for a spacecraft that hadn't been built yet. There was a whole visual language around that moment that felt genuinely excited about the future. Lightweight, geometric, a bit translucent, confident without being loud about it.

It wasn't just games. The Designers Republic were doing it. Early Wipeout. Consumer electronics packaging that looked like it came from somewhere ten years ahead. The thing those all had in common was that they managed to feel flat and alive at the same time. Not "minimal in the current sense" but minimal in the sense that nothing was wasted, but everything was still allowed to have personality. That combination stuck with me more than almost anything else I encountered growing up, and I've been trying to understand why ever since.


## The mark

The symbol started somewhere else entirely. My family runs a small business, and at some point there were attempts at rebranding it. Sketches, directions, things that got developed and then shelved. Most of it went nowhere. But one form kept coming back, kept getting reworked, and at some point during that process it stopped feeling like it belonged to the brief and started feeling like it belonged to me.

The letterforms shifted over time, got abstracted, lost their original reference and picked up a new one. *olo* is the last syllable of my name, and if you look at the mark long enough you can see where it came from. Two forms that still carry the memory of letterwork in their curves, two anchoring shapes at the base holding the whole thing together. It doesn't explain itself. It just sits there and holds.

Monochrome teal. No outline, no variation. Same mark at any size.

The rounded square. Not quite a circle, not a sharp corner. The radius shifts slightly depending on context: bigger on a nav icon, tighter on a button, almost nothing on a tag. But it's always the same basic shape. Map pins, tool cells, status dots, the cursor, the nav icons. Same shape, different scale. It comes straight from that era of HUD design where everything was a slightly softened rectangle.

### Lines

Every border is the primary teal at reduced opacity. No grey in the system, no separate structural color. The grid is just the teal turned down. One 1px line does everything: section breaks, card edges, table rows, the nav rail. It sounds like a small decision but it's what makes the whole thing feel like one object rather than a layout with pieces sitting on top of it.

Syne is the display face. Heavy, geometric, tracking pulled tight. It's the part that has a voice. IBM Plex Mono handles everything else: labels, stats, nav items, anything that's communicating information rather than making a statement. They never share the same level of hierarchy. When one is big the other is small. The contrast between them does all the work a third typeface would need to.

Both are free fonts. The right choice doesn't have to be the expensive one.

Two states. The primary is the only element where the teal runs at full opacity as a fill. The secondary has no fill, just the border and a muted label that picks up the accent on hover. The 4px corner keeps it in the same shape family as everything else without going soft.

### Cursor

The default cursor is gone. In its place, a 10px teal dot and a 36px ring at 45% opacity that lags slightly behind the dot. The dot marks exactly where you are. The ring makes every hover state feel physical without needing a separate visual indicator. Moving across the page leaves a teal trace.

### Navigation

The rail sits at 48px at rest, just a column of small icon squares. Hover it and it opens to 72px, the labels appearing as vertical mono text. Active items fill solid and glow faintly at the edge. Same geometry, same color, same logic as everything else. The nav doesn't feel like a separate component because visually it isn't one.

On mobile it becomes a bottom pill. Same squares, but the active item stretches out and shows the full label instead of the initial. Same idea, smaller space.

## What it's trying to do

Everything is flat. No gradients, no shadows. But nothing sits still either. Hover something and it reacts. Scroll past the map and the dots move. The cursor follows you around the page. The nav opens when you get close.
That's what I liked about that era. It wasn't just how things looked. It was that they felt like they knew you were there.
